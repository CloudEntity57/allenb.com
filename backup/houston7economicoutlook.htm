HOUSTON UPDATE - December 17, 2004
“How Will This Recovery Continue”
With interest rates rising and consumer confidence waning, more analysts are beginning to question how the national economic recovery can be sustained. Analysts from Wall Street to Pennsylvania Avenue are lowering their forecasts for 2005, expecting GDP growth to decline over 2004's performance by a full percentage point or more.

The primary focus of all these dismal forecasts is upon the consumer. The consumer is worried about the future, cringing at every piece of bad news from the Middle East. They are strapped for cash, having loaded up their credit card debt to the limit and having exhausted their home equities to pay for past excesses. Furthermore, they soon will be worrying about that home equity which is likely to shrink over the next several years instead of constantly refilling their Midas cups of perpetual wealth. The bottom line is that the consumer is not going to be of any significant help in promoting nation-wide growth. While it is unlikely, with the labor market improving, that the consumer will totally become a shrinking violet, they are likely to spend in short spurts, covering holiday and back-to-school obligations and otherwise limiting themselves to bargains.

So what’s going to keep the U.S. economy from tumbling? Where’s the strength going to come to buoy the economy up in the absence of consumer strength? It will come from several places.

However, let’s first look at some positive influences upon the consumer that will keep them from completely dying on the vine. The most important will be falling energy prices. This will return billions of dollars back into consumer’s pocketbooks. Second, is job growth. The current 1.5% national rate of job creation will add more than 1.5% to real dollar spending since real per capita income will also be growing (though rather meagerly) In a world of 2.5% inflation, that alone should generate increases in retail sales of at least 4.0%. That’s not very impressive, but it is at least positive.

The really encouraging news, however, comes from the other sectors of the national economy. The decline in the dollar will be an even greater boon to U.S. exports next year than this year. Ah, the cries of our trading partners in Asia and Europe ought to be music to our ears. It means that the dollars decline is beginning to hurt abroad, and that’s exactly what it needed to do. U.S. exports are pushing up towards all time highs and the numbers will be much more impressive next year. In addition, the national preoccupation with bargains will keep industry constantly at work to lower costs. That will translate into more productivity enhancing technological investment. Business investment experienced a nice rebound in 2004 and the upward trend will continue through next year. Look for growth in the double digit range again. Finally, the economy will continue to benefit from the excessive amount of fiscal stimulus emanating from Washington. While that’s bad in the long-run, it continues to a be positive in the short-run. So, don’t write off the economy quite so soon. If you’re bent on worrying, focus more on 2006 and 2007.

Dr. Barton Smith
Director

HOUSTON UPDATE - November 20, 2004

“The Short-Term Future for the U.S. Economy”
 

The Houston Chronicle reported after this fall’s symposium that we were suggesting the likelihood of a recession within the next couple of years. While that possibility cannot be totally ruled out, the tone of the Chronicle article was somewhat more pessimistic than we intended.   The risk of a recession stems from the vulnerability of U.S. consumers who never retreated from their spending spree during the past recession.  Thus, they remain over-extended and excessively dependent upon continued rising home equities, a phenomenon that is unlikely to occur.  Indeed, we spent a considerable amount of time at the symposium explaining why a housing bust in this nation’s most inflated markets was virtually assured. Furthermore, because this bust would affect nearly a third of all Americans, it would have serious negative implications to the national economy.  While we indicated that the ills of these markets were not significantly present in Houston, we also warned that Houston would still be indirectly affected by the induced national slowdown such a housing market correction would have on the overall local economy. But, in our entire presentation we never mentioned the “r” word, recession. (See the symposium report, “The Changing Face of Houston - Implications for the Future”)

 While we recognize that the housing market has the potential to create a mild national recession such as occurred in 1991, at this point we think it is unlikely for several reasons.  Helping to ameliorate the impact of a housing bust will be the significant benefits of softening energy prices, further declines in the dollar, and the continuing fiscal stimulus of federal government spending. It is also true that the coming economic environment, characterized by weak consumer spending stimulated only by “bargains” and by stiff competition within the global economy, will continue to promote a rebound in high tech, cost-cutting technology.  All of these together should help to compensate for the wealth losses associated with home price declines as long as they remain moderate (10 to 25% in those most vulnerable markets).   Thus, we are only forecasting a slowdown in the national growth rate by 2006 or 2007, not a recession.   How severe that slowdown becomes greatly depends upon the application of wise monetary policy which at this point unfortunately can not be counted on.  Once again, the FED needs to monitor a national asset market (the home market this time instead of the stock market) as much as overall macro economic statistics.  Our worry stems from the fact that each time the Greenspan FED has faced this challenge they’ve failed. (See Director’s Comments on this web site)   We are among many that believe that the housing market correction need not produce a recession if wise monetary policy is pursued. We’ll have to wait and see.  

Dr. Barton Smith
Director
Institute for Regional Forecast
