IT’S BETTER THAN IT LOOKS

March 30, 2004
Recently the news headlines indicated that 1,600 jobs were gone from the Houston economy as though this were new news. However, while that headline’s numbers were accurate, the message was misleading. For better or for worse, we all must rely on the employment numbers produced by the Bureau of Labor Statistics and its cohort, the Texas Workforce Commission. Earlier this month they revised Houston’s employment total downward by about 14,000 jobs, strangely deducting almost all of that revision from the July, 2003's total. However, other than the quirky way they allocated the changes, the new BLS/TWC numbers actually make more sense than the original ones.

In many ways the new employment series doesn’t change the general historical picture of the Houston economy dramatically, but it now shows that the first half of 2003 was worse than we were at first led to believe and the second half was actually somewhat better. Yes, it is true that in comparison with February 2003 employment in Houston was 1,600 less last month (February 2004). But too many treat urban statistics like this as though they have the same meaning and interpretation as the national data we all hear about every month. However, it just isn’t so.

National data typically compares employment on a month to month basis. Thus, we know how many jobs were created just last month. That’s good up-to-date data. The BLS can do this with national data because the data, based on the large national sample, is stable enough and because they have years of data which allow them to seasonally adjust the numbers. Thus, the next time you hear a news report on national employment, the number reported will be a seasonally adjusted number.

On the other hand, all local employment data is unadjusted. Thus, month to month comparison’s can be very confusing. In Houston we always add 12 to 15 thousand jobs each November and December for Christmas. In January those jobs all go away. In June or July we lose a substantial amount of local government employment from area schools. In September we get them back again.

To avoid the seasonality confusion most analysts simply compare the most recent month’s statistics with the numbers from the same month a year ago, the presumption being (which is not always perfectly correct) that you are comparing apples and apples (March against March). Of course, the problem with these 12 month job growth numbers is they don’t really tell you much about what’s happening right now, and today this has never been more true for Houston. Yes, Houston’s employment is 1,600 less than last February, but the real story is that Houston shed about 25,000 jobs during the first half of 2003, only to gain all but 1,600 of those jobs back during the past 6 months. That paints a very different picture of Houston’s current economy than the simple headline that 1,600 jobs are gone.

The fact is that the data show Houston’s employment picture improving rather dramatically, much more so than the national economy. Since August, Houston employment has increased approximately 1.25%. That translates into an average annualized growth rate of 2.59%. That’s not bad at all. In fact, that’s much better than we anticipated last November at our annual Forecast Symposium. I’m not arguing yet that the Houston economy is advancing at full throttle, but it is certainly making progress, better than anticipated, better than the nation, and certainly better than the simple 12 month comparative story would leave you to believe. For more information on the current direction of the Houston economy, see our discussions in DATABook Houston and the May release of our Spring 2004 Symposium report.


Dr. Barton Smith
Director

Houston is among the nation's fastest-growing and most diverse metropolitan areas.

Between the '90 and the '00 censuses, the population of the Houston Consolidated Metropolitan Statistical Area (CMSA) grew 25.2 percent, according to the Census Bureau. The nation's population increased 13.2 percent over the same period.

Net migration accounts for 49 percent of Houston's population growth since '90. Given Houston's prominence in international business, it is perhaps not surprising that foreign immigration accounts for an overwhelming 78 percent of net migration (some 451,000 people) since '90.

Houston, which prizes its ethnic diversity as a source of strength in a global economy, is becoming still more diverse. The '00 census found that no racial or ethnic group now constitutes a majority of the metro area population; by '30, under reasonable scenarios, Hispanics could become a majority. Asians - nearly 5 percent of the population in '00 - will climb to 10 percent within the next two decades.

Among the nation's largest metropolitan areas, the Houston-Galveston-Brazoria CMSA ranks fifth in Hispanic population (1,457,800 in '02, according to Sales & Marketing Management) and 10th in Asian & Pacific Islander population (248,700). Census data put Hispanics at 1,348,588 and Asians & Pacific Islanders at 227,909 in '00.

LARGE SUBURBAN CITIES IN WEST ARE FASTEST GROWING

WASHINGTON, D.C. (U.S. Census Bureau) – Large suburban cities in the West dominated the list of America's fastest-growing cities, according to population estimates released by the U. S. Census Bureau.

Between April 1, 2000, and July 1, 2002, Gilbert, Ariz., was the fastest-growing of 242 cities with populations of 100,000 or more.
 
The estimates show no change in the rankings of the ten largest cities since Census 2000. The ten largest U.S. cities as of July 1, 2002, in order of population were New York, Los Angeles, Chicago, Houston, Philadelphia, Phoenix, San Diego, Dallas, San Antonio and Detroit.
Of the ten  largest cities, Phoenix and San Antonio grew the fastest from 2000 to 2002, followed by San Diego, Houston and Los Angeles.
 
Austin was the only one of Texas’ 20 largest cities to lose population between 2000 and 2002. Its population shrank by 1,100.

Houston is expected to outperform the U.S. over the next quarter-century.

During '02-'30, The Perryman Group sees population growth averaging 1.54 percent annually in the Houston region. Over the same period, this prominent Texas-based economic forecasting firm expects employment to rise at a 1.94 percent annual rate.

Employment growth faster than the nation's is predicated in part on disproportionately rapid growth in economic base employment, especially in energy-independent sectors. Says M. Ray Perryman, Ph.D., the firm's president: "The area's traditional dependence on energy-related business is…projected to continue to decrease over this period. [Houston's] economic expansion…is fueled by its proximity to the Gulf of Mexico, as well as by the important role the area plays as a major manufacturing, shipping, and tourism center. Global economic growth will greatly benefit [the area's] export and import enterprises."
