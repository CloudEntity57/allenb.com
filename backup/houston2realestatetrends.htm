LAND PRICES SOAR INTO STRATOSPHERE  May 3, 2005
 
COLLEGE STATION, Texas (recenter.tamu.edu) – If land prices were on fire last year, they skyrocketed into space this year, says Dr. Charles E. Gilliland, research economist with the Real Estate Center at Texas A&M University. A typical tract of land sold for $1,273 per acre in 2004. A year ago, a typical tract sold for $1,097. In other words, land prices appreciated 16 percent in 2004.
 
Prices were up in all regions of the state. The greatest appreciation statewide was in the Canadian Breaks, where prices were up 41 percent. In the Coastal Prairie-South they were up 38 percent; in the Panhandle North, 37 percent; and in the Piney Woods-South, 35 percent.
 
The market did not take a breather in 2004, as 7,833 land sales were reported to the Center, an 8 percent increase in sales volume compared with 2003. Sales of properties of all sizes soared, with small property prices up 29 percent and typical-size property prices up 19 percent statewide.
 
The latest Center research shows that farmland values respond to nonfarm factors more than to agricultural factors. >From 1986 to 1993, farming had the most influence on land prices. Today, agricultural lending groups are saying there is not much of a relationship between agricultural production and land prices. Data indicates that is the case. However, there was a high correlation (95.5 percent) between personal income not related to agriculture and land prices from 1993 through 2004. 
 
Gilliland projects that if the current conditions persist, land values will appreciate 10 percent in 2005, reaching $1,380 per acre, and by 2010, prices will be 57 percent higher than 2004 prices, reaching $1,969 per acre.
 
The latest land data was presented at the 15th Annual Outlook for Texas Land Markets last week. Next year’s conference will be held on April 27–28, 2006. For the Texas Land Market Developments Third Quarter 2004, see http://recenter.tamu.edu/pdf/1716.pdf.

 

 

1/3/2003 Real estate remains bright spot in city's economy
  With the population in the metro area anticipated to rise by nearly 447,000 over the next five years, and with the expansion of business operations predicted to continue, the real estate market should see significant increases, according to economist M. Ray Perryman, Ph. D., president and CEO of The Perryman Group in Waco. Economic forecaster Barton Smith has a similar view. "Most area real estate markets will enjoy a better environment in 2003. The exception will be the single-family residential market. Much of the increased occupancy in the apartment market, however, will come at the expense of lower growth in single family occupancy," says Smith, director of the University of Houston's Institute for Regional Forecasting.

Single Family Residential

Houston home sales fly off the charts - 4/16/2004
Houston area Realtors® recorded the highest number of pending home sales ever for a single month in March, and total sales of single-family homes increased by almost 24 percent to 5,057, up from 4,081 recorded last March. In addition, Houston's 2004 first quarter sales surpassed 2003’s first quarter sales by 13.8 percent. According to the Houston Association of Realtors'® Multiple Listing Service (MLS), total property sales in March, consisting of single-family homes, town homes, multi-family homes, country homes, high-rise properties and lots listed by Realtors®, topped out at 6,146, representing the highest number of total sales ever recorded during March. "Houston's residential real estate market continues to perform month after month; this is great news for Houston's economy," said HAR Chairman Ted C. Jones. "However, consumers should keep in mind that the MLS system serves as an aggregate for all of Houston as well as its surrounding counties. Sales may be brisk in one area and not necessarily the other." Available inventory in the greater-Houston area also escalated. Realtors® listed approximately 12.4 percent more properties in the MLS system last month compared to March 2003. There are some 39,660 Realtor® listings in the system, with 4,519 sales slated to close during the next month. The median price of single-family homes reached $130,430 in March, the highest median price ever recorded for that month.
[Real Estate Center Online News]

HOUSTON APARTMENT MARKET UPDATE  May 3, 2005
 
HOUSTON (O’Connor & Associates) – In first quarter 2005, more than 1,500 Houston apartment units were absorbed. Almost 6,000 units were absorbed in the past 12 months. Fourteen new properties have gone up in the past three months, adding almost 3,500 units to the market. Another 48 projects, totaling 11,390 units, are currently under construction.
 
Officials say that because of aggressive development, occupancy dropped almost 2 percentage points to less than 87 percent in the first quarter. Developers are favoring the Bayou City, and construction is projected to outpace the high levels seen in the past two years.
 
Rents were nearly stable at close to 80 cents per square foot for the quarter. About 14,200 jobs were added in March 2005 and annual job growth is up 1.2 percent. 

 

Houston apartment market - 11/14/2003
Overall the multifamily market in Houston remains soft but there are some encouraging signs for the future – an increase in gross domestic product, somewhat higher mortgage rates and non-negative job growth. The volume of calls from prospective renters has been steadily rising, according to leasing agents. Absorption of units was up in the quarter with 299 units absorbed, but for the past 12 months absorption is down 1,078 units. Occupancy was down to 90 percent overall in the third quarter. Rents were up 78 cents per square foot in the third quarter. Construction of new units will continue to hurt occupancy levels into 2004, as 7,100 units were delivered to the market in 2003 and 19,088 are under construction. Apartments are a relatively attractive investment option. Low interest rates and high appreciation have increased demand for multifamily properties.
[Real Estate Center Online News]

 

5/12/2003 Houston building boom
  According to M/PF Research, Houston is experiencing the nation's largest jump in apartment building, where ongoing construction as of January 2003 (10,436 units) jumped a whopping 73 percent from the level recorded in January 2002 (6,043 units). Of the product currently under way, half (5,377 units) is scheduled to come on stream in 2003's first six months. Though new completions are expected to slow in the year's latter half, construction has already begun on more than 3,000 units with completion dates targeted for sometime in 2004. On top of that, multifamily permit statistics suggest that a large chunk of future product is on the horizon. Permits were issued for approximately 10,800 multifamily units during 2002, nearly doubling the number of units approved in 2001 and ranking third in the nation. Construction is currently heaviest in the Katy/Southwest Houston submarket, where building has been initiated on 2,524 units. A particularly active cluster in this submarket is the neighborhood just south of the Energy Corridor but west of Eldridge Parkway, where three apartment communities that each top 300 units (Camden Oak Crest, Villas at West Oaks and San Brisas) are in process. Another 270-unit complex (Bridges of Eldridge) is under construction nearby. However, Houston's apartment market has struggled as of late. According to M/PF Research's 4th quarter survey, the Houston area experienced net move-outs of approximately 5,110 units during the October-December 2002 time frame, dropping occupancy 1.5 points from the September level to register at 92 percent as of 4th quarter. Though recent economic forecasts predict an improved job market in 2003, the metropolitan Houston area is unlikely to see a considerable improvement in apartment market conditions, as occupancy is expected to inch up 0.1 point to 92.1 percent by December 2003.

Retail construction to shrink this year - 3/5/2004
Retail construction in the Houston area is expected to fall by almost 30 percent this year as the market absorbs space generated by strong growth over the past few years, according to an annual survey by the Wulfe & Co. realty firm. This year, a projected 3.06 million square feet of new retail space will open, down from 4.35 million in 2003 and 6.8 million in 2002. Super discount department stores make up 32 percent of projected retail growth, with Wal-Mart opening four SuperCenters, and Target building three new stores. Ten new supermarkets will go up: six by H-E-B and four by Kroger. The report also noted that the free-standing corner drug store concept pioneered by Walgreens will keep popping up throughout Houston. No new Home Depot stores or Lowe’s are planned for 2004, but several of each are planned for 2005. One of the retail market’s biggest challenges is what to do with the increasing number of closed stores, including K-Mart, Lord & Taylor and some Garden Ridge stores, as well as vacancies created by relocations. There are several ways to fill those spaces. In the case of 43 Albertson’s stores that became vacant after the national grocer pulled out of Houston, rival grocers took over a vast majority of those locations, while 99 Cents Only acquired the distribution center. In other instances, landlords can find unconventional, non-retail tenants. For example, a large electronics store near The Galleria became a Houston Community College branch. According to the report, the parts of town that will see the most retail growth are the Northwest Corridor, which includes U.S. 290, Texas 249 and the towns of Tomball and Magnolia, as well as The Woodlands.
[Houston Chronicle]

 

2/25/2003 Houston's retail market
  At the end of 2002, the Houston retail market reported an overall occupancy of approximately 86 percent, down slightly from year-end 2001’s occupancy rate of 86.8 percent. Occupancy has been helped by retailer expansions as well as a slowdown in retail construction. For 2002, construction totaled approximately three million square feet, down significantly from 2001’s approximate six million square feet of new construction. The majority of space was accounted for by the construction of multitenant community centers with grocery store anchors, as well as large-format tenants such as Kohl’s and SuperTarget and junior anchors such as Office Depot and Ross Dress for Less. Houston’s healthy occupancy situation looks stronger considering store closings that occurred during 2002. Kmart closed 11 stores as part of its bankruptcy, Albertsons left the market and Weiner’s closed 25 locations. Auchan also announced in early 2003 that it is closing its two Houston locations. As of year-end 2002, asking rental rates for Houston-area retail projects remained stable, although they typically were not showing increases. Concessions were available in some instances. Rental rates at year-end 2002 were in line with rates at mid-year, with Class-A space ranging from $18 to $20 per square foot per year in the better markets for small-tenant in-line space. Rates for well located outparcels were $25 or more per square foot. For second-generation space in good locations, rates typically were in the $15 to $16 per-square-foot range. Class-C space typically ranged from $9 to $12 per square foot.

Office

Rate of deterioration tapered off for Houston's office market in 2003 - 1/30/2004
While Houston’s office leasing market endured the blow suffered from the recession and subsequent slower-than-expected economic recovery, supply and demand fundamentals staggered with a dramatic rise in vacancy caused by a brittle office employment base. Due to the so-called jobless recovery, the prolonged economic downturn significantly depressed tenant demand pushing vacancy to its highest level since 1994. Meanwhile, business contractions coupled with space completions added nearly 4.6 million square feet of vacant space onto the market and magnified vacancy by 2.4 percent to 19.7 percent. Consequently, the vacancy increase caps a two-year swing that has seen the balance of power in the office market shift from landlords to tenants, who now find multiple options for premium space at a discount garnished with generous concession packages and open-handed financial incentives. The CBD posted a monumental tenant exodus during 2003 when large corporate evacuations flooded the market with sublease space and over 1.5 million square feet of new product. With nearly 8.5 million square feet currently sitting dark, downtown vacancy is projected to climb upward again this year as the addition of the former Enron Tower further dilutes the already watery tenant demand. On the bright side, there are tentative signs that the worst may be over for the suburban office market resulting in an improved outlook by the close of 2004 for Houston’s outlying office markets. Although net absorption was down by more than 327,000 square feet within the suburbs last year, this pressure was by far an improvement over 2003, when negative absorption totaled 1.9 million square feet. The citywide sublease inventory will ease over the next 12 to 18 months while construction activity will be constrained, providing a welcome relief from the high levels of new supply that hit in recent years. Since the commercial real estate market typically lags the overall economy by six months to a year, rents are likely to remain soft through at least the first-half of this year before firming up much later in 2004.
[Houston Business Journal]

 

5/9/2003 Houston office losing tenants at slower pace
  Houston’s office overall vacancy rate moved up a full point in first quarter 2003 to 18.2 percent, the highest since 1995. The prolonged economic downturn has depressed demand and allowed high vacancy rates to become commonplace. Houston’s office market had negative absorption of 315,021 square feet in the first quarter. This is an improvement over last year, when negative absorption averaged 727,000 square feet per quarter. Uptown-Galleria area had the greatest increase in vacant space as Phillips Petroleum Company vacated 260,000 square feet of sublet space. Tenants are taking advantage of market conditions by upgrading their space, locking in lower rents and negotiating longer lease terms.

Industrial

Overall industrial market occupancy declines - 2/20/2004
In fourth-quarter 2003, Houston industrial market occupancy was lower overall (87.85 percent) from the previous quarter, and rent growth remained steady at 38 cents per square foot, according to O’Connor and Associates’ Houston Industrial Performance Update. The report notes that speculative construction around the Beltway and in the northwest and southwest has brought large amounts of inventory on the market, depressing occupancy levels. Occupancy is particularly low for buildings finished in the past five years, which tend to have higher rental rates. It adds, however, “The long-term outlook for these markets far outpaces the short-term prognosis. For now, it is a buyer’s/tenant’s market, but as we ride out the tough times, the balance will begin to shift by late 2004.”
[Houston Business Journal]

 

6/1/2003 Southwest market strong
  After building and leasing up all of the 262,000 square feet of warehouse and office space in the first phase of Beltway 8 Business Park, the developer is expanding it. The market for industrial space in the southwest part of Houston is strong, with an overall occupancy rate over 90 percent. Northwest Houston has been the area of choice for warehouse developers over the past decade. Industrial development on the southwest side has been more restrained, and the market is tight. Vantage Cos. Is constructing three buildings, with a total of 100,000 square feet of space, in the Beltway 8 Business Park.
